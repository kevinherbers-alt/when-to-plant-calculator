How to Find Your Frost Dates

Nearly every garden timing decision keys off two dates: your average last spring frost and your average first fall frost. They define your growing season, they're the inputs every calculator on this site works from, and they're specific to your location — sometimes down to the neighborhood. Here's how to find yours and what the numbers really mean.

Where to look them up

The fastest route is searching "last frost date" or "first frost date" plus your zip code — several gardening sites maintain lookup tools built on national weather station data. For the most authoritative local answer, check your county or state cooperative extension office's website; extension services publish frost tables for their regions and often note how local geography shifts the dates. Seed companies' planting guides and the various almanac sites use the same underlying weather-station records, so they'll generally agree within a few days.

What "average" actually means

A frost date is a statistical middle, not a promise. The commonly published figure is the date with roughly a 50% chance that frost is finished (or has arrived, for fall). In other words: in half of years, frost happens after your average last-frost date. Some sources also publish 10% and 90% probability dates, which are worth a look — the gap between them can be three or four weeks, and it tells you how volatile your local springs are.

The practical takeaway: treat the average date as the center of a window, not a green light. For cheap, replaceable plantings like a row of beans, planting right at the average date is a reasonable gamble. For a tray of tomato seedlings you've nursed for eight weeks, waiting a week or two past the average — or watching the 10-day forecast — is cheap insurance. That's also why the when-to-plant calculator builds a post-frost buffer into transplant timing for tender crops.

Microclimates: why your yard may differ

Official dates come from weather stations, and your garden isn't a weather station. Cold air flows downhill and pools in low spots, so valley-bottom gardens frost earlier in fall and later in spring than the published dates. Urban areas, south-facing slopes, and beds near masonry walls or large bodies of water run warmer. A few seasons of watching your own garden — or a $10 min/max thermometer — will tell you whether to mentally shift the official dates a week in either direction.

Putting the dates to work

Once you have both dates, the whole season falls into place: the last spring frost drives seed-starting and transplant timing in the when-to-plant calculator, and the first fall frost sets your planting deadlines in the fall planting calculator. The stretch between them is your frost-free growing season — worth knowing as a single number too, since crops like watermelon or winter squash need 100+ frost-free days to be worth attempting.
