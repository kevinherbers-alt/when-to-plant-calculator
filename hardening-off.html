How to Harden Off Seedlings Before Transplanting

Seedlings raised indoors live a pampered life — steady temperatures, gentle light, no wind. Move them straight into full sun and open weather and even healthy plants can scorch, wilt, or stall for weeks. Hardening off is the simple process of acclimating them gradually, and it's the step most often skipped by first-time seed starters. It costs about a week and saves the whole transplant.

Why indoor seedlings need it

Two things make indoor plants fragile outdoors. First, leaves grown under grow lights or windowsill light have never experienced full-strength sun; direct UV bleaches and burns them the same way a first beach day burns skin. Second, stems that have never felt wind are weak — outdoor gusts snap or flatten them. Gradual exposure lets the plant thicken its leaf cuticle and strengthen its stems before facing the real thing.

A simple 7–10 day schedule

  1. Days 1–2: Set seedlings outside in a shaded, wind-protected spot for 2–3 hours, then bring them back in.
  2. Days 3–4: Extend to 4–5 hours with an hour or two of gentle morning sun.
  3. Days 5–6: Half a day outside with several hours of direct sun; a light breeze is now a feature, not a threat.
  4. Days 7–8: Full day outdoors in close to their final conditions; bring them in only if nights are cold.
  5. Days 9–10: If nights are staying above roughly 50°F (45°F for cool-season crops), leave them out overnight. After a night or two outside, they're ready to transplant.

Watch the plants, not just the calendar: pale or bleached patches mean too much sun too fast — drop back a step for a day or two. Wilting usually just means they need water more often outside, where sun and wind dry pots quickly.

Timing it with your transplant date

Count backwards: if your transplant window opens May 15, start hardening off around May 5. The when-to-plant calculator gives you the transplant window for each crop from your frost date — and if you're not sure of that date, see how to find your frost dates. Cool-season transplants like broccoli and cabbage harden off in cooler conditions and tolerate the process earlier in spring; tender crops like tomatoes, peppers, and basil should never be out when temperatures dip below the mid-40s°F.

Shortcuts and safeguards

A cold frame makes hardening off nearly automatic — open the lid a little more each day. A spot under a patio table or a shade-cloth-draped bench works as the "partial sun" stage. And transplanting on a cloudy, calm day (or in the evening) gives even well-hardened seedlings the gentlest possible first night in the ground.
